# Avalanche Safety Guide

## Definition & Hazards
- **Event:** Mass of compact snow or rock suddenly breaking away from a steep slope.
- **Velocity:** Often exceeds 100 km/h.
- **Triggers:** Spontaneous (weight, temperature changes) or External (human movement, vibrations).
- **Risks:** Engulfs path, heavy weight (tonnes), high casualty potential.

## Risk Indicators
- **Terrain:** Steep slopes, rapidly accumulated fresh snow.
- **Signs:** Cracks in snow surface, thumping noises, breaking up of snow surface.

## Survival Protocol (If Caught)
- **Evade:** Run perpendicular (to the side) to the path; avoid outrunning the avalanche.
- **Hold On:** Grip sturdy objects (boulder, large tree) if possible.
- **Breathe:** Keep face above surface. Create an air space by spreading arms in front of the mouth.
- **Mobility:** "Swim" toward the surface to stay in open air; thrash arms to avoid sinking if necessary.
- **Visibility:** Raise one arm to signal rescue teams.
- **Physiology:** Remain calm; avoid hyperventilation to preserve oxygen levels.
- **Post-Risk:** Seek immediate medical help after extraction due to hypothermia and air deprivation risks.

## Required Equipment
- **Transceiver:** Avalanche Victim Locator (AVL) for locating buried victims.
- **Tools:** Shovel and probe for extraction.
- **Recommendation:** Carry all equipment when in avalanche terrain.

## Prevention & Risk Management
- **Guidelines:** Do not venture off-piste without a professional guide.
- **Information:** Consult daily avalanche bulletins from local authorities.
- **Compliance:** Adhere to rules and recommendations from local authorities and ski resorts.
- **Mitigation:** Resorts may trigger controlled avalanches and conduct slope patrols to manage risk.

## Emergency Contact
- **Number:** 112 (Mountain Rescue).
- **Action:** Keep emergency numbers accessible at all times.
