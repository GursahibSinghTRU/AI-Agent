# Heat Stress Safety Summary

### ⚠️ Why It Matters
Elevated heat at outdoor workplaces poses a significant risk for heat-related illnesses. Even on hot, sunny days, workers and students must be vigilant to prevent heat stress.

### 1. Recognizing Heat Stress Symptoms
Monitor yourself and others for the following signs:
*   **Physical Changes:** Extremely high body temperature, hot/dry skin, pale or bluish skin, and muscle cramps.
*   **Vital Signs:** Increased heart and respiration rates.
*   **Neurological/Behavioral:** Confusion, hostile behavior, dizziness, weakness, or feeling intoxicated.
*   **Dehydration Indicators:** Throbbing headache, nausea/vomiting, dark-coloured urine.

### 2. Immediate Response
If you or a co-worker suspects heat stress:
*   **Seek Medical Aid:** Immediately get medical attention.
*   **Cool Down:** Move to a cool, shaded, or air-conditioned environment.
*   **Hydrate:** Drink plenty of cool water immediately.
*   **Remove Clothing:** Loosen or remove unnecessary clothing to aid cooling.
*   **Apply Coolant:** Fan the worker and spray them with cool water.

### 3. Prevention Strategies
Implement these guidelines to avoid heat stress during fieldwork or outdoor tasks:
*   **Hydration:** Drink water frequently (approx. 1 cup every 15–20 minutes).
    *   *Check:* Ensure urine is clear, not dark.
    *   *Avoid:* Drinks with high caffeine or sugar content.
*   **Work Scheduling:** Plan physically demanding jobs for morning or evening when temperatures are cooler.
*   **Acclimatization:** Allow workers' bodies to adjust to hot environments gradually over time.
*   **Rest:** Take extra breaks in cool areas during demanding tasks.
*   **Attire:** Wear light-colored, loose-fitting, breathable clothing (e.g., cotton) and avoid synthetic fabrics.
*   **Manpower:** Use relief workers or assign extra staff for physically demanding roles.
