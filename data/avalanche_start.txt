# Avalanche Safety Guidelines

## Avalanche Terrain
- **Slope Angle:** Most avalanches occur on slopes between 30° and 45°.
- **Terrain Features:**
  - **Steep Slopes:** Slopes larger than 10m x 10m (tennis court size) are at risk.
  - **Convex Rolls & Cornices:** Act as trigger points; areas under or on these features are high-risk.
  - **Avalanche Paths:** Identified by forest openings (tree gaps) or alpine slope shape. Remain aware of slopes above your location even on flat terrain.
- **Terrain Traps:** Features that increase consequence severity if caught include gullies, creek beds, ditches, cliffs, trees/rocks in slide paths, and flat areas under steep slopes.

## Environmental Conditions
- **Heavy Snowfall:** ≥30cm in 48 hours or ≥2cm per hour for several hours.
- **Wind:** Creates dangerous wind slabs on the lee or downwind side of ridges and high features. Surface lines indicate drifting.
- **Warming:** Strong sunshine, warm temperatures, and rain destabilize snowpack. Warming close to 0°C after a storm is a common trigger window.

## Signs of Snowpack Instability
- **Shooting Cracks:** Cracks shooting from under weight indicate unstable snow.
- **Recent Avalanches:** Witnessed activity or physical signs of recent sliding.
- **Whumpf:** Audible sound of snowpack collapse indicating layer instability.
- *Note:* Absence of signs does not guarantee safety.

## Avalanche Forecast
- **Danger Rating:** Primary indicator found at the top of the forecast.
  - Categories: Low, Moderate, Considerable, High, Extreme.
- **Coverage:** Provided per elevation band for a specific region.
- **Validity:** Covers the current day and the next two days.

## Recommended Actions
- Always check trailhead signs regarding avalanche terrain.
- Complete the **Avy Savvy** online tutorial.
- Enroll in an **Avalanche Canada Training** course.
- Monitor forecasts before planning trips.
