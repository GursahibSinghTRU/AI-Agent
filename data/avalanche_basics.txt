# Avalanche Safety Fundamentals (Risk Mitigation)

**Risk Context**
- **Definition:** Avalanches are masses of snow moving down a slope.
- **Statistics:** Approximately 27 U.S. fatalities annually; risk applies to all winter backcountry activities (skiing, snowboarding, hiking, climbing).
- **Disclaimer:** Education is required; internet content is supplemental only.

**The Avalanche Triangle**
An avalanche requires three specific factors; removing any eliminates the risk:
1. **Unstable Snow:** Influenced by weather, precipitation, and wind. Consult forecasts for unstable layers.
2. **Avalanche Terrain:** Slopes between 30° and 45° where snow can slide.
   - **Terrain Traps:** Gullies, cliffs, crevasses, rocks, and trees increase injury severity and entrapment depth.
   - **Connected Slopes:** Gentle terrain connected to unstable slopes above retains risk.
3. **Trigger:** Human weight (travelers) or natural forces (wind, rain). Note: Noise/shouting does not trigger avalanches.

**Pre-Trip Protocols**
- **Forecast Check:** Review daily avalanche danger ratings (Low, Moderate, Considerable, High, Extreme) via regional backcountry avalanche centers.
- **Mandatory Gear (Per Person):**
  - Avalanche Transceiver (Beacon)
  - Snow Probe
  - Snow Shovel
- **Training:** Complete an avalanche awareness course before entering the backcountry. Practice rescue skills regularly.

**Field Warning Signs (Red Flags)**
Avoid activity in areas exhibiting:
- **Recent Avalanches:** Visible debris or crown lines indicating recent slides.
- **Shooting Cracks:** Visual signs of snowpack collapse.
- **Whumpfing:** Audible noise caused by air release from collapsing snow.
- **Weather Events:** Recent rain, heavy snowfall, strong winds, or rapid temperature changes.
- **Persistent Slabs:** Unstable layers buried under newer snow, often fatal long after storms.

**Risk Management Strategy**
- **Prevention:** Education and decision-making are primary tools; rescue gear is a last-ditch measure.
- **Decision Making:** If unstable snow is present, avoid avalanche terrain. If avalanche terrain is necessary, wait for stable snow or reduce triggers.
