## Condensed Knowledge Base Entries

### 1. Water Activity Risk Assessment Checklist

## Pre-Activity Site Safety Assessment
- [ ] Water depth is known and appropriate (max: chest-depth for shortest participant)
- [ ] Water visibility is clear enough for underwater observation
- [ ] Flow speed is low (<= half adult walking speed for rivers)
- [ ] Boundaries are secure and highly visible
- [ ] No sharp objects, dangerous animals, or microbial hazards present
- [ ] Water temperature is safe for planned activity duration
- [ ] Emergency exit routes are identified and accessible
- [ ] Rescue equipment (throw ropes, buoyancy aids) is on-site and functional
- [ ] Communication method for emergency services is confirmed
- [ ] Weather forecast reviewed; contingency plan for sudden changes

### 2. Supervision Ratios for Student-Led Water Activities

| Activity Type | Max Participant:Supervisor Ratio | Notes |
|--------------|----------------------------------|-------|
| Basic swimming instruction | 5:1 | In-water supervision required |
| Land-based water safety training | 10:1 | Classroom or shoreline setting |
| Open water recreation (calm) | 8:1 | Participants must demonstrate basic swimming competency |
| Moving water/whitewater | 4:1 | Additional certified rescue personnel required |
| Participants with disclosed medical conditions | 2:1 or 1:1 | Individual risk assessment required |

### 3. Emergency Action Plan Template (Adapted for Campus Use)

## TRU Water Activity Emergency Protocol

### Immediate Response Sequence
1. Recognize: Identify distress signals (vertical position, inability to call for help, gasping)
2. Alert: Activate emergency response (campus security: 250-828-5000; 911 if off-campus)
3. Rescue: Use "Reach-Throw-Row-Go" hierarchy:
   - Reach: Extend pole, branch, or clothing from stable position
   - Throw: Toss buoyant object with attached line
   - Row: Use boat only if trained and conditions permit
   - Go: Enter water ONLY as last resort with proper PPE and backup
4. Resuscitate: Begin CPR if victim is unresponsive and not breathing normally
   - Compression rate: 100-120/min; depth: 5-6 cm (adults)
   - Ratio: 30 compressions : 2 rescue breaths
   - Continue until professional help arrives or victim recovers
5. Report: Document incident via TRU Safety Reporting System within 24 hours

### Roles and Responsibilities
- Activity Leader: Primary decision-maker; maintains headcount; initiates emergency protocol
- Designated Responder: Trained in water rescue/CPR; positioned for optimal observation
- Communications Lead: Manages emergency calls; provides location coordinates; liaises with responders
- Student Support: Assists with crowd control; provides comfort to other participants

### 4. Participant Screening Guidelines

## Pre-Enrollment Health and Risk Screening Questions
(To be completed confidentially by participants or guardians)

1. Do you have any medical conditions that could affect your safety in or near water? 
   (e.g., epilepsy, asthma, heart conditions, seizures, diabetes)
2. Have you ever experienced a loss of consciousness, seizure, or blackout?
3. Do you have difficulty with mobility, balance, hearing, or vision that could impact water safety?
4. Are you currently taking medications that may cause drowsiness, dizziness, or impaired judgment?
5. Do you have any concerns about your ability to follow safety instructions in a group setting?

If "Yes" to any question: 
- Schedule confidential consultation with campus health services
- Develop individualized accommodation plan
- Determine if modified participation or alternative activities are appropriate
- Document accommodations and obtain informed consent

### 5. Training Competency Standards for Student Leaders

## Minimum Certification Requirements for Student Activity Leaders

### Core Competencies (All Water-Adjacent Activities)
- [ ] Valid Standard First Aid and CPR Level C (or equivalent)
- [ ] Completion of TRU Water Safety Orientation Module
- [ ] Demonstrated ability to conduct pre-activity risk assessments
- [ ] Knowledge of local water hazards (Thompson River currents, cold water shock, etc.)

### Additional Requirements (Swimming Instruction or High-Risk Activities)
- [ ] Lifesaving Society Bronze Medallion or equivalent
- [ ] Completed "Train-the-Trainer" module for peer education
- [ ] Annual refresher training on rescue techniques and CPR
- [ ] Documented supervised practice hours (minimum 10 hours)

### Refresher Schedule
- CPR skills: Review every 6 months; formal recertification every 12 months
- Rescue techniques: Annual practical assessment
- Risk assessment protocols: Biannual review with campus safety office

### 6. Cultural and Accessibility Considerations

## Inclusive Water Safety Programming

### Addressing Sociocultural Barriers
- Offer gender-segregated training sessions where requested
- Provide modest swimwear options and private changing areas
- Use visual aids and multilingual materials for international students
- Engage cultural liaisons when introducing water safety concepts to communities with limited water exposure

### Accommodations for Disabilities
- Use flotation aids to enable participation for students with mobility limitations
- Adapt skill assessments: focus on "any method to reach safety" vs. specific stroke techniques
- Ensure communication methods accommodate hearing/vision impairments
- Train staff on disability-inclusive rescue techniques (e.g., safe transfer methods)

### Consent and Communication
- Obtain informed consent in participant's preferred language/format
- Clearly explain inherent risks without causing undue alarm
- Provide opt-out alternatives that maintain educational value

### 7. Incident Reporting and Quality Improvement

## Post-Incident Documentation Requirements

### Mandatory Data Fields
- Date, time, location, and environmental conditions
- Participant demographics (age, skill level, disclosed conditions)
- Activity type and supervision ratio at time of incident
- Sequence of events (timeline with witness statements)
- Response actions taken and timing
- Outcome (injury severity, medical treatment, follow-up)
- Contributing factors identified

### Continuous Improvement Cycle
1. Quarterly review of incident reports by Campus Safety Committee
2. Annual audit of training site assessments and emergency plans
3. Feedback loop: Update training materials based on near-miss analysis
4. Share de-identified learnings with partner institutions via BC Safety Network

## Key Evidence-Based Standards from WHO Document

### Target Populations
- School-age children aged 6 years and older for swimming instruction
- Pre-school children aged 1-4 years for day-care supervision
- Bystanders aged 10 years and older for rescue/resuscitation training

### Critical Ratios
- Swimming instruction: maximum 1 instructor per 5 participants
- Day-care (ages 1-4): maximum 1 caregiver per 13 children
- Day-care (under 12 months): maximum 1 caregiver per 3 infants
- Rescue training (land-based): maximum 1 instructor per 10 trainees
- Rescue training (water-based): maximum 1 instructor per 5 trainees

### Curriculum Requirements
- Swimming skills: ability to swim 25 metres using any method
- Floating competency: maintain airway above water for minimum 30 seconds
- Rescue techniques: non-contact methods only (reach, throw) for untrained rescuers
- CPR training: include both rescue breathing and chest compressions; refresher every 12 months

### Site Safety Standards
- Water depth: shallow enough that shortest participant stands with water below armpits
- Water visibility: clear enough for underwater observation of participants
- Flow characteristics: river current no faster than half adult walking speed
- Boundaries: secure, highly visible, and physically enforced where needed
- Temperature: safe for planned activity duration and participant vulnerability

### Documentation Requirements
- Written emergency action plan integrated into instructor training
- Incident reporting system capturing adverse events, activations, and outcomes
- Site safety audits conducted regularly by qualified personnel
- Participant records including consent, screening results, and skill progression
- Instructor qualifications, training dates, and ongoing competency assessments

### Safeguarding Measures
- Community involvement in staff selection where formal background checks unavailable
- Written policies for reporting and investigating abuse or neglect concerns
- Ongoing monitoring of instructor-participant interactions
- Training for all staff on recognizing signs of abuse, neglect, or exploitation
- Clear protocols for managing disclosures and engaging statutory authorities
